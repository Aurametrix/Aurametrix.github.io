Bioinformatics

 

Purpose of the Course

 

This course is a graduate level exploration of bioinformatics.  It is a stand-alone, computer-based course for biology students wanting to know how bioinformatic tools are used by investigators in biology.  Students should acquire a working knowledge of several major bioinformatics applications and databases by the end of the course.

 

Course Description

 

This is a four-unit graduate course for students in the Masters of Biotechnology program.  It is a problems-centered, hands-on course held in an SJSU computer lab.  The focus of the course is on computational  identification and analysis of DNA and protein molecules, their properties, and cellular pathways.

 

Course handouts can be found at  www.biology.sjsu.edu/facultystaff/gabashvili.aspx

Reading and Problem Solving

 

The 2006-2007 course text, Bioinformatics: A Practical Guide to the Analysis of Genes and Proteins edited by Andreas Baxevanis and Francis Ouellette, was chosen because of its focus on molecular analysis as would be done by a biologist, and its breadth and depth of coverage, as well as its discussion of challenges faced by users of particular tools.

 

The book’s website (http://www.wiley.com/bioinformatics) provides additional information related to material discussed in the book.

 

This year course will be based on instructor’s notes and web materials, but Baxevanis and Ouellette’s book is still the main text.

 

There will be five problem sets over the course of the semester.  Solving these will be essential to the mastery of the subject and the development of confidence in your ability to use bioinformatics in your future work. Project will help to develop independent research skills.

 

Course Calendar

 

The course syllabus shows the dates of each class meeting, the particular topic of focus each day, reading assignments from the course text, and dates each problem set and the class project is due.

 

Course Requirements

 

Prerequisites for the course is either an upper division cell biology course (such as Bio 135), an upper division development course (such as Bio 105) OR an upper division molecular genetics course (such as Bio 116) OR biochemistry (such as Chem 135).

 

Evaluation

 

Student performance will be based on grades on five problem sets (15% each), one project (20%) and a final exam (20%).  The lowest grade earned on problem sets will be dropped so that total possible score is 100%.

 

Collaboration with other students in the course is highly encouraged at all times except for during the writing up of problem set solutions and, of course, during the final exam.  Online discussion, group study and peer review of writing are each welcome forms of cooperation.

 

See the campus Academic Integrity Policy in the SJSU catalog (page 460, and online) for definitions of plagiarism and cheating and for student rights and instructor options in the event of the violation of the SJSU policy.

How to Use the Syllabus

 

The syllabus identifies the day of the semester on which each topic will be explored, and the reading that will be relevant to that that topic.  Dates that problem sets and the project are due, and the date and time of the final exam is also given.  It is a good idea to transfer these dates to your personal planner / calendar and also to schedule in extra time to do any necessary catch up.

 

How to Study for this Course

 

It is recommended that you read the assigned reading before the appropriate lecture, take note of questions you have or specific topics you feel unclear on, ask questions in class when the topic comes up or the questions appear relevant, come to office hours as often as you can, particularly if you have any questions or any topics that you are confused about, and solve problems related to the ‘lecture’ topic as soon after as possible.  Schedule sufficient time to read and to solve problems – four hours per week for each or these activities is probably enough.

 

Learning Tools

 

The following additional resources may also be useful.  On reserve at the library: Bioinformatics (Higgins & Taylor) and Bionformatics for Dummies (Claverie & Notredame).  Both of these has been used as the course text in previous years.  The library has additional texts for longer term checkout.

There is also a short tutorial on molecular biology online at NCBI that is quite useful:  http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/About/primer/index.html

 

And the NCBI “bookshelf” has electronic (and searchable) versions of several useful molecular biology and bioinformatics texts: http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/entrez/query.fcgi?db=Books

More Instructor Information

 

My graduate work was done under supervision of Professor Alexander Grosberg, then a visiting professor at MIT. I computationally modeled bacterial viruses, infection and viral assembly processes. My next endeavors were in the area now known as genetic epidemiology. The human genome project was still in its early stages, but many features and traits had already been recognized as familial, so I mined epidemiological data for the modes of inheritance. I was also computationally simulating the proliferation of cells and the formation of chromosomal aberrations in health, aging and disease, particularly due to the altered DNA repair machinery. Several years later, I returned to this subject, computationally interpreting experimentally visualized dynamics of chromosome movements in mitosis.

From January 1995, I was a visiting scientist with the University of Québec, studying mechanism of plant photosynthesis by FTIR spectroscopy in the laboratory of professor Mario Fragata. Later, I returned to studies of viruses, employing a wide range of molecular biological, biochemical and biophysical techniques in the laboratory of Professor Philip Serwer. In particular I discovered new mechanisms of gene expression regulation in bacteriophage T7, occurring on the level of translation and thus involving the ribosome. I started to thoroughly study this fascinating molecular machine producing proteins for each and every cell in 1997, in the laboratory of Joachim Frank, a pioneer of single-particle image reconstruction for cryo-electron microscopy. I was involved in computational structural biology research, reconstructing and interpreting the three-dimensional images by modeling, docking and fitting structures of protein and RNA components, contributing to the development of biomage processing software SPIDER. Next, I applied informatics approaches to study the ribosome and the mechanism of translation in the laboratory of Russ Altman, Stanford University, where I worked from 2001 to 2003. My Stanford projects included mining of biochemical data for structure related information and knowledge engineering approaches to linking phenotypes and genotypes. In 2003-2006, I was the technical lead of Computational Biosciences Research Program at Hewlett-Packard Laboratories in Palo Alto, California. I worked in the areas of bio-nanotechnology, data management, and genomics of age-related hearing loss, collaborating with Harvard University’s Hearing Group.  I was teaching informatics in early 90s, guest-lecturing molecular biology, genetics and bioinformatics at several Universities since 2001, teaching biomedical informatics and telemedicine at Computer information Systems and Engineering Technology programs of DeVry University in 2006-2007. I work at Ingenuity Systems based in Redwood city.
