Search:

Exact match, wildcard (*) and multiple term searches are supported.

For wildcard * searches, place a (*) before of after a string, e.g. oxal*.

The following terms can be searched in IPA: Systematic Name, IUPAC Name, CAS Registry Number, Chemical Formula and Brand Name, Entrez gene name, HUGO gene symbol, Affymetrix Expression Probeset IDs, SNP ID, Agilent, Applied Biosystem, Codelink, Entrez Gene (LocusLink), GenBank, GenPept, GI Number, Illumina, International Protein Index, RefSeq, Swiss-Prot Accession, and UniGene.

Searching for terms in more than one category will conduct an AND search operation. For example, if you search for kinases (in the Molecule Types dropdown menu) and invasion, all genes that are kinases involved with invasion are returned. You can restrict your search by adding subcellular location - say, "cytosol", from the subcellular locations

Ctrl-H (Help > Workflows > Search Workflow instructions)

Functional Annotation:

Easiest way to find what all your molecules have in common is to a list and run functional analysis (right click, or click on options arrow). This will bring a list of categories. Clicking "show functions" will show specific functions and diseases. You can arrange them by p-value to select the most relevant functions and diseases

If your molecules are in the pathway, use overlay tools: Overlay > Functions and Diseases. Use "show functions" or expand categories (+) to see the details.

Exploring pathways:

Use build tools to connect molecules in pathways, explore path from one molecule to another, add molecules that either regulate, are regulated by, or that physically interact with your molecule. You may specify the molecule types and relationship types that you are most interested in seeing. You may specify whether you want to see upstream, downstream, or both types of interactions. For example, Build > Grow, grow out all molecules that are "upstream", relationship type: "activation" will help you find all kinases that are upstream of a particular transcription factor. Direct interactions appear in the network diagram as a solid line, whereas indirect interactions appear as a dashed line.

Ctrl-W (Help > Workflows > Pathway Workflow instructions)

Upload data:

New > Upload Dataset, or File > Upload Dataset, or Ctrl-U, or right click on the "Data Set files" under your project
Browse files on your computer, Select the dataset file and click the Open button.
During upload, IPA reviews the column headers and selects a format based on its best guess.
If your identifiers were not in the first column or were not guessed correctly, specify the identifiers by yourself, from the "Select Identifier Type" drop down menu
Choose the observaion value keeping in mind that automatic cutoffs will be applied, unless your observation is assigned to "other"
For example, fold change implies that you are not interested in changes less than 1

Analyze your data:

Core Analysis allows you to interpret datasets in the context of biological processes, pathways and molecular networks. If you have multiple datasets that represent multiple timepoints or dosage treatments, use Comparison Analysis to understand which biological processes and/or diseases are relevant to each timepoint or dose.

Tox analysis allows you to assess toxicity and safety of compounds of interest early in the development process and rapidly understand the relevant toxicity phenotypes and clinical pathology

You can use Tox Functions and Tox Lists together to help understand and delineate the pharmacological response, mechanism of action, and mechanism of toxicity in liver, kidney, and heart as induced upon drug treatment.

For example, let's say you are interested in studying Compound A, which will be used to treat a particular condition. You've treated rats at a high-range dose for 1 day, 3 days, and 5 days. You want to know if the rats are exhibiting any side effects indicating hepatotoxicity. After running microarray experiments on the liver, you can use IPA to determine if the molecules that are up- or down-regulated in this liver tissue are involved in functions relating to liver toxicity.

First look at the Tox Functions results. If tox functions associated with liver have the lowest p-values, this is the organ that will be affected the most. Customize the chart to only look at Hepatotoxicity functions. Here we see that at 1, 3, and 5 days molecules that were affected are involved in liver hepatomegaly. At 3 days, molecules that were affected are involved in liver hepatitis. At 5 days, molecules that were affected are involved in liver steatosis.
